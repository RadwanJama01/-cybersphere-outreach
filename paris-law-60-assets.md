# ANGLE 3: MISTAKE — What Almost Went Wrong

---

## Asset 3.1 — LinkedIn Post (Mistake Angle)

We almost destroyed a law firm's reputation with our AI receptionist.

I'm dead serious. Three days before launch, our voice agent said something to a test caller that would have been a compliance nightmare if it had gone live.

Here's what happened and why it matters for anyone building AI for regulated industries.

We were building the AI receptionist for Paris Law Group, a personal injury firm in NYC. The system was working beautifully in testing. Natural voice through ElevenLabs. Smart conversation handling through Claude. Clean automation through Make and GHL. We were feeling confident. Maybe too confident.

During one of our final test calls, a tester pretended to be a potential client describing a workplace injury. They asked, "Do you think I have a case?"

The AI said yes.

That's a problem. A massive problem. In most states, telling someone they "have a case" can be construed as giving legal advice. An AI receptionist giving legal advice is an ethical violation that could get attorneys sanctioned, disbarred, or worse. It could create an implied attorney-client relationship with someone the firm has never actually evaluated.

We caught it because we were running structured adversarial testing — not just "does the call flow work" testing, but "what's the worst thing this agent could say" testing. The kind of testing that most agencies skip because it's tedious and expensive.

The fix wasn't simple prompt tweaking. We rebuilt the entire response framework for legal boundary questions. The agent now redirects with something like: "I can't evaluate the specifics of your situation — that's what the attorneys are here for. What I can do is get you a consultation so they can review everything with you directly. Would tomorrow morning work?"

It's warm. It's helpful. It doesn't give legal advice. And it still moves the caller toward a booking.

But that wasn't the only near-miss. We also caught the agent:

Asking a caller for their Social Security number when it wasn't needed. The training data included intake forms that sometimes collect SSNs, and the AI was mimicking that unnecessarily. We stripped it.

Offering to "transfer to an attorney" at 2am when no attorney was available. The agent now accurately represents who's available and when.

Making statements about case timelines that could be misleading. "These cases usually settle in 6-12 months" is the kind of thing a human intake coordinator might say casually, but coming from an AI representing the firm, it's a liability.

Every single one of these would have been fine for a restaurant booking bot or a dental appointment scheduler. But law is different. Healthcare is different. Financial services are different. When you're building AI for regulated industries, the margin for error is essentially zero.

This is why I push back hard when people say "anyone can build AI agents." Sure, anyone can build a demo. Building something that won't create legal liability for your client in a regulated industry? That requires understanding the domain, the regulations, and the ways AI systems fail.

My time at Stanford studying computer science and my years doing ML engineering at Databricks didn't just teach me how to build systems. They taught me how to think about failure modes. How to design testing frameworks that catch the problems you don't expect. How to think about edge cases that never show up in demos but always show up in production.

We caught every one of these issues before launch. Paris Law Group went live with a system that's been running clean for weeks. But it's a reminder: in AI, what you prevent matters as much as what you build.

# ANGLE 4: DECISION — The Key Call That Changed Everything

---

## Asset 4.1 — LinkedIn Post (Decision Angle)

One decision changed the entire trajectory of our AI project for Paris Law Group.

We chose to build one call flow instead of five.

That might sound obvious. It's not. And almost every AI agency gets this wrong.

When Paris Law Group came to us, they wanted everything automated. New intake calls. Existing client check-ins. Scheduling. Vendor calls. Internal routing. Five distinct call flows, each with their own logic, edge cases, and failure modes.

The natural instinct — and what most agencies would do — is say yes to everything. Scope it all. Charge more. Deliver a system that handles it all from day one.

We said no.

I've watched this movie before. At Databricks, I saw enterprise ML projects die because teams tried to solve five problems at once and ended up solving zero. The same pattern plays out in AI agent development.

Here's what happens when you build five call flows simultaneously for a law firm: you spread your testing thin. Each flow gets maybe 40 test calls instead of 200. Edge cases slip through. The voice tuning that works for an empathetic intake call sounds wrong for a vendor scheduling call. # ANGLE 8: LESSON — What We'd Do Differently

---

## Asset 8.1 — LinkedIn Post (Lesson Angle)

If I could rebuild the Paris Law Group AI receptionist from scratch, I'd change three things.

The system works. The results are excellent. But now that it's in production handling real calls, I can see what I'd do differently. I'm sharing this because the AI space has too many "everything went perfectly" stories and not enough honest retrospectives.

Here's what I'd change.

First, I'd start testing with real callers sooner.

We ran 200 simulated test calls before going live. That was the right volume but the wrong composition. All 200 were people who knew they were testing an AI agent. That changes behavior. Real callers don't say "I'd like to report an automobile accident that occurred on the corner of Fifth Avenue and 42nd Street." They say "Hey, um, so I got hit by a car? And my leg is really messed up and I don't know what to do."

Real callers pause. They cry. They ramble. They ask questions out of order. They say "hold on my other line is ringing" and disappear for 30 seconds. Simulated testers, no matter how good, don't replicate this fully.

If I did it again, I'd run 150 simulated tests for the basic flow and compliance issues, then go live in a controlled beta — maybe handling 20% of after-hours calls while a human backup monitors in real time. We'd catch the real-world conversation patterns faster.

Second, I'd build the analytics dashboard from day one.

We focused so hard on making the AI work that we didn't build proper monitoring until after launch. For the first week, reviewing performance meant listening to call recordings manually. That's fine for 47 calls. It doesn't scale.

Now we have dashboards tracking call volume, qualification rates, booking rates, average call duration, sentiment indicators, and edge case frequency. We should have built this from the start. The data from week one would have been valuable for optimization and it would have given the client confidence from day one.

Third, I'd involve the client's intake team earlier in conversation design.

We designed the conversation flow based on observed calls and our own expertise. It was good. But when the human intake team started hearing the AI's work, they had feedback that would have been gold during the design phase. Things like: "We always ask about the date of the incident in the first two minutes because of statute of limitations concerns" and "When someone mentions they've already talked to another attorney, we handle that differently."

These nuances existed in the calls I observed, but some were so subtle I missed them. If I'd had the intake coordinator co-design the conversation flow, we would have captured those patterns from the start instead of adding them in post-launch iteration.

Here's the meta-lesson: technical excellence isn't the same as implementation excellence. The system was technically sound. The voice was great. The architecture was robust. But the implementation — the process of building, testing, and deploying — had room for improvement.

This is what I mean when I say we do things differently at Cybersphere. We actually look back, analyze, and improve. We don't just declare victory and move on. The Databricks culture of rigorous post-mortems and continuous improvement stuck with me. Every project makes the next one better.
